# Asset 1: The Founder's Letter

*For the About page, the pitch deck opening slide, media requests, and any context where the personal origin story is appropriate.*

---

Dear friend,

I've spent years in payment processing. I know how the money moves. Every time a small business owner runs a card — a barbershop in Memphis, a taqueria in Phoenix, a hardware store in Atlanta — a few cents of every transaction flow through a pipe. That pipe leads to interchange, to processors, to sales agents, and eventually to people like me.

For a long time I accepted that as just how it works.

Then I started asking a different question: *What if the pipe could go somewhere else?*

Not instead of paying agents their fair share — they earned it. Not by asking merchants to give more — they're already working hard. But by redirecting what's already mine — a portion of our company's retained margin — toward the people and causes doing real good in the neighborhoods where those transactions happen.

The more I sat with that question, the more I couldn't let it go. There's a word for that kind of persistence. I believe it's calling.

What I've seen in this industry is that small business owners want to give back. I've heard it hundreds of times. A restaurant owner who wants to support the food pantry two blocks away. A barbershop that sponsors the Little League team. A gym owner who wants to help the youth center afford a new floor. The desire is real and it's everywhere.

But the infrastructure for generosity doesn't exist. There's no automatic way to make giving happen from commerce. There's no trusted system that connects a merchant's transactions to a vetted local cause. There's no place where a consumer can shop locally and know — really know, with a public ledger entry to verify it — that their purchase made something good happen.

TheGiveback is that infrastructure.

We built it on a simple principle: the infrastructure for generosity should be as automatic as the infrastructure for commerce. The pipes for moving money already exist. We're just redirecting a portion of the flow.

Twenty to thirty percent of our own company's margin — not merchants' rates, not agents' residuals, our own — routes automatically to vetted foundations serving the communities where our merchants operate. Every month. Without anyone having to decide, remember, or ask.

And then we went further. We built a marketplace where consumers can find and shop at certified merchants. A card that earns points toward local giving. A learning platform for people who want to build businesses. A live events layer where artists perform and the proceeds fund causes. A sports program where a hardware store's daily transactions fund the Little League team it sponsors. A technology layer that measures whether foundation programs actually work, not just whether money arrived.

All of it connected. All of it automatic. All of it honest — every penny traceable from the moment it leaves a merchant's terminal to the moment it arrives at a foundation's account.

I started with a question about where the money goes. I ended up building a platform for what a community can become when commerce and generosity move together.

That's what we're doing. I hope you'll be part of it.

Chris Humphrey
Founder, DataOne / TheGiveback.org

# Asset 2: The Problem Statement — The Gap Nobody Has Filled

*The strategic gap analysis. For media pitches, foundation presentations, investor conversations, and partnership outreach. Explains what the platform solves before explaining what it builds.*

---

## Small Businesses Want to Give Back. The System Makes It Nearly Impossible.

### The Gap

Walk into almost any independent small business in America and ask the owner whether they want to give back to their community. The answer is almost always yes.

Not as a marketing strategy. Not to attract customers. Because they live in the neighborhood. Because they know the people in it. Because their kids go to the same schools. Because they understand, better than most, that a strong community is what keeps a small business alive.

The desire is genuine and widespread. The infrastructure to act on it is almost entirely absent.

Here is what a small business owner faces today when they try to give back systematically:

**They don't know who to trust.** Charities vary wildly in quality. There is no easy way for a hardware store owner to know whether the nonprofit that sent them a donation request actually serves the neighborhood effectively, or whether overhead costs consume most of what they give, or whether the organization even exists in a meaningful way.

**There is no automatic mechanism.** Every dollar a business gives today requires a conscious decision, a separate transaction, and an administrative act. It doesn't happen unless someone makes it happen. In the chaos of running a small business, it consistently doesn't happen — not because the owner doesn't want to give, but because giving has no infrastructure.

**They can't prove it happened.** When a business does give, there's almost no way to demonstrate it publicly in a verifiable, credible way. A canceled check is not a story. A social media post claiming "we give back" is not evidence. Consumers are right to be skeptical — cause marketing has been so frequently abused that authentic giving is indistinguishable from performance.

**The impact is invisible.** Even when a business does give, they almost never find out what happened. They know money left their account. They don't know whether it funded a meal, a mentorship session, a youth program, or a building fund. The connection between the transaction and the human outcome is broken.

**Their giving doesn't compound.** A business that makes a one-time donation to a local food pantry generates a one-time benefit. Nothing about that transaction builds toward the next one. There's no network effect, no amplification, no story that gets richer over time.

### What Consumers Are Missing

On the consumer side, the gap is equally real.

Consumers broadly want to support local businesses. Survey after survey confirms it. "Shop local" resonates as a value. But most consumers can't answer a simple question: *if I buy my coffee at an independent shop instead of a chain, what difference does it make beyond keeping a local business profitable?*

The answer should be concrete and traceable. It isn't. There's no infrastructure that lets a consumer see, in specific terms, what their local spending generates for their community.

There's also no trusted directory of businesses that have made verifiable giving commitments — not claims on a website, but actual publicly traceable disbursements to vetted organizations.

And there's no mechanism for a consumer who wants to do more — add a small contribution at checkout, round up their purchase, direct their loyalty card points to a local cause — to do so without downloading a separate app, creating a new account, and remembering to use it.

### What Foundations Are Missing

Local foundations and nonprofits operate on funding that is almost entirely unpredictable. Grants are competitive, seasonal, and require extensive application processes. Individual donations are sporadic. Government funding is subject to political cycles.

The one funding source that could be systematic, predictable, and genuinely local — the commerce of the businesses and consumers they serve — is completely untapped. No infrastructure connects the transaction volume of a neighborhood's small businesses to the nonprofit organizations serving that same neighborhood's needs.

There's also no objective, third-party measurement of whether foundation programs actually work. Funders receive self-reported metrics, overhead ratios, and testimonials. None of this is independently verified. None of it tells you whether a financial literacy workshop actually engaged its participants, or whether a youth mentorship program actually changed anything, or whether the money that arrived actually produced a measurable outcome.

### The Specific Gap in Numbers

US families spend an estimated $40 billion annually on youth sports alone. Youth sports organizations consistently list finding local business sponsors as their most challenging fundraising task. 67% of youth sports organizers report this difficulty — yet they also rank it as their most successful fundraising method when it works.

The small business local sponsorship relationship — where a hardware store sponsors a Little League team, a restaurant puts up a banner at a youth soccer field, an auto shop funds uniforms — is the most authentic and community-rooted form of local commercial giving that exists. And it fails routinely because there is no infrastructure to make it systematic, measurable, or sustainable.

### What's Never Been Built

Nobody has built a three-sided system that simultaneously:

- Gives merchants an automatic, transparent, zero-effort mechanism for routing a portion of their commerce to vetted local causes
- Gives consumers a verified directory of those merchants and a way to participate in the giving through their own shopping
- Gives foundations predictable, recurring funding with objective measurement of whether their programs actually work
- Gives sports teams a permanent funding stream tied to real commerce rather than seasonal campaigns
- Gives creators and artists a live events platform where their performances directly fund community causes
- Gives learners — particularly entrepreneurs in underserved communities — the education infrastructure to build the businesses that generate all of this

TheGiveback is that system. Built on the payment processing infrastructure that already touches every transaction. Designed to make the infrastructure of generosity as automatic as the infrastructure of commerce.

The desire to give has always been there. The infrastructure just didn't exist.

Until now.

You sponsor the Little League team. You let the food pantry put a collection box on your counter. You give a discount to the church down the street when they need something for their event. You've always done these things because you live here. Because your customers are your neighbors. Because you know what it means to a community when local businesses show up.

But here's what happens every time: you give, and then nothing. No receipt that means anything. No way to know what happened. No story to tell. And somewhere in the back of your mind, the question you've never quite been able to answer: *does it matter?*

It matters. We just built the infrastructure to prove it.

**What changes when you become Giveback Certified:**

You process payments exactly the way you always have. Nothing about your rates, your equipment, or your routine changes. But underneath every transaction, a small calculation is running. A youth development program. A housing organization. The cause you would choose if someone handed you a check and asked you to decide.

At the end of every month, your dashboard shows you exactly what happened. Not an estimate. Not a projection. The actual dollar amount, the actual foundation, the actual disbursement — traceable to a public ledger anyone can verify.

Then the platform generates a post for you. Specific, honest, shareable. "$412 went to the Midtown Food Pantry this month — from 847 transactions at Miller's Hardware. That's approximately 91 meals." You can post it to Instagram in one tap.

You were already doing this. You were already giving back in every way you knew how. We just made it automatic, specific, and provable.

A certification badge your customers recognize — on your window, your website, in the app where they're already looking for local businesses to support. A dashboard that shows you, in real time, the good your business is generating. Marketing content that writes itself every month, specific and honest in a way no ad agency can manufacture. A marketplace storefront where your neighbors can find you specifically because they want to support businesses that give back. A sports team sponsorship program where your daily transactions fund the Little League team you've been sponsoring for years — automatically, verifiably, and with a story the whole neighborhood can see.

And one more thing, the most important: the answer to the question you've been carrying.

Yes. It matters. Here's the proof.

*For foundation outreach, grant-writing conversations, and nonprofit network partnerships.*

## Funding That Doesn't Require You to Compete for It

You know the cycle. You write the grant. You wait. You find out in February whether you'll have funding in June. You build programs around the hope that the money arrives. When it doesn't, you cut. When it does, you scramble to spend it by the deadline or risk losing it.

You've built something real. You're serving real people with real needs. And you spend a third of your year asking for permission to keep doing it.

There's a different kind of funding you've never had access to: the commerce of your own community.

Every small business in your neighborhood is processing payments. Every swipe at the hardware store, every order at the restaurant, every service at the barbershop generates a small margin that flows to the payment processor. For the first time, a portion of that margin can flow to you — automatically, monthly, and without you writing a single word of a grant application.

TheGiveback is a payment processing platform that routes 20–30% of its retained margin to a vetted network of foundations. When a certified merchant in your neighborhood designates your organization as their matched cause, their daily transaction volume generates a monthly disbursement to your operating fund. No campaign. No competition. No deadline. Just commerce, running continuously, funding your work.

**What you receive:**

Predictable monthly income you can plan programs around — not react to. A public impact page where the community can see exactly what their commerce is generating for your work. Objective program measurement from AI-powered tools — not to judge you, but to help you improve: here's where your session held attention, here's where you lost the room, here's what the data suggests you change. A 90-day rolling forecast so you can hire a part-time staff member, sign a lease, or commit to a supply contract because you can see what's coming.

And a community that can verify your work. Not self-reported metrics. Not testimonials. Actual engagement measurements from actual sessions, published publicly, letting your donors and your community see that what you do works.

**What we ask:**

That your organization meet our vetting standard — 501(c)(3) in good standing, reasonable financial health, mission aligned with the communities where our merchants operate. That you publish a specific monthly outcome post showing what the disbursement funded. Not a thank-you note. A real account: "This $2,847 funded 632 meals, one refrigeration repair, and a new serving station at our Friday distribution."

We ask for honesty and specificity in return for funding and a platform.

You've been doing the work. We built the infrastructure to fund it.

We believe every dollar that moves through a small business carries a choice.

Not a visible choice. Not one that requires any extra step or any extra cost. Just a quiet fork in the road that most payment infrastructure never acknowledges: this money could go somewhere that matters.

For decades, the margins from small business transactions have flowed in one direction: from the merchant, through the processor, into residuals and profits and overhead. The merchants kept serving their communities. The money kept flowing away from them.

We built a different pipe.

Not by taking from anyone. Not by asking merchants to give more than they can afford, or by guilting consumers into charity, or by treating generosity as a marketing function. By redirecting a portion of what we earn — our margin, our profit — to the foundations, the sports teams, the schools, and the community organizations doing real work in the neighborhoods where our merchants operate.

We built a marketplace where shopping locally is verifiable giving. A live events platform where a musician's performance funds a food pantry. A technology layer that measures whether community programs actually work — not just whether money arrived.

We believe a nonprofit doing real work deserves to know, specifically, what works — and to receive funding that doesn't require them to compete for it every six months.

We believe small businesses want to give back. They just need the infrastructure.

We built it.
