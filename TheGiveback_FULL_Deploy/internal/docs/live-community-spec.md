## 1. Feature Overview & Philosophy

### The Core Idea

TheGiveback Live and the Community Layer are not separate products. They are two extensions of the same mission: making giving visible, personal, and felt — not just logged. Together they answer the question every merchant, artist, artist fan, and foundation supporter eventually asks: *"What did this actually do?"*

Live events create the community assembly moments where giving feels real in real time. The community layer is the verified testimony record that answers "what did this actually do" with proof rather than claims. Together they form a flywheel: events generate giving moments, giving moments generate content, content attracts more people to events and to certified merchants, more people generate more giving moments.

### Why This Is Different From Every Other Platform

The critical design insight: **the content on TheGiveback's community layer is generated by verified giving events, not by asking people to post about giving.**

On Instagram, a merchant saying "we give back to the community" is a caption. On TheGiveback, it is a ledger entry anyone can verify. That distinction changes what social interaction means on this platform. When a fan taps "I witness this" on a merchant's giving post, they are attesting to a verifiable fact, not endorsing a claim. When a consumer taps "I was there," it is backed by their transaction history. This is something no social platform — Instagram, Facebook, TikTok, Twitter — can offer, because none of them have the payment data underneath.

### What These Features Are Not

- Not a general social network competing with Instagram or Facebook
- Not a live streaming platform competing with Twitch or YouTube
- Not a silent auction platform competing with GalaBid or OneCause
- Not a place to post daily life content
- Not a discovery algorithm or viral content engine

They are a **verified testimony layer** for a community that is already giving, and a **live event infrastructure** that creates the moments worth testifying about.

## 2. TheGiveback Live — Virtual Concerts & Events

### Concept

Any certified merchant can host a live stream event on TheGiveback platform. A musician, artist, or performer streams a show. Fans attend from home — free or ticketed. During the show, fans tip. At the end, a silent auction closes. Every dollar that flows through the event is processed by DataOne and split three ways: artist, foundation, platform.

The merchant is the host. The artist is the performer. The foundation is the third beneficiary. This is the ecosystem assembled into a single live moment.

### Four Event Formats

#### Format 1: Free with Tipping
- Artist streams for free, anyone can join with no payment required
- Fans tip in any amount during the show
- Three-way split applies to every tip
- Use case: discovery, community building, new artist onboarding
- Equivalent to: a busker on a street corner, but the hat goes to a cause

#### Format 2: Ticketed with Tipping
- Fans purchase a ticket ($1–$100, artist sets price) to access the stream
- Fans can tip on top of ticket price during the show
- Three-way split applies to ticket revenue and tip revenue separately
- Ticket revenue split: 70% artist / 20% foundation / 10% platform
- Use case: main recurring format for established artists
- Equivalent to: a club show, but a fifth of every ticket goes to the community

#### Format 3: Silent Auction Alongside Stream
- Same as Format 2 (free or ticketed) with an auction sidebar running simultaneously
- Auction items listed by certified merchants from the merchant dashboard
- Auction closes at show end — winning bids processed through DataOne
- Auction proceeds: DataOne acquiring margin + covenant 20–30% of that margin to foundation
- Use case: charity benefit shows, quarterly foundation fundraisers, merchant showcase events
- The artist becomes the live auctioneer — they can see the auction status in real time on their performer dashboard

#### Format 4: Recurring Community Shows
- Merchant sets up a regular show schedule (weekly, monthly) with the same or rotating artists
- Fans subscribe for $5–$20/month to access all shows in the series
- Subscription revenue: 70% artist / 20% foundation / 10% platform
- Foundation's 20% accumulates monthly alongside regular covenant giving — shown as a separate line in the foundation's disbursement
- Merchant's impact dashboard shows the show's contribution as its own line item
- Use case: "Miller's Hardware presents Local Acoustic Night — every Friday" style recurring events

#### Artist Registration
- Simple onboarding form: name, bio, genre, payment method, chosen foundation (defaults to merchant host's matched cause — artist can redirect to any vetted foundation in the network)
- No approval gate — any individual can register as a performer

- Artist's profile page shows their upcoming shows, past show giving totals, and the foundation(s) they've supported

## 3. Silent Auction Module

### Standalone Auction (No Live Show Required)

Silent auctions can also run without a concurrent live stream — a merchant can run a 48-hour or 7-day online auction from their storefront at any time. This is the simpler entry point before a merchant is ready to host a live show.

#### Creating a Standalone Auction
- Merchant creates auction from their dashboard: start date, end date, items, minimum bids
- Foundation selection: defaults to merchant's matched cause
- Items: merchant's own products/services + invited items from other certified merchants
- Share: custom QR code and URL for the auction, embeds on the merchant's website

#### Bidding Mechanics
- Mobile browser — no app download required for bidding (app provides enhanced experience)
- Auto-bid: bidder sets a maximum, system bids incrementally up to that limit
- Outbid notification: SMS alert within 60 seconds of being outbid
- Final 10 minutes: auction extends by 5 minutes if any bid is placed (prevents sniping)
- Countdown timer visible on every item card
- Minimum bid increment: $5 (configurable by merchant, minimum $1)

#### Settlement
- Winning bidder's card charged immediately at auction close
- All bids processed through DataOne — acquiring margin + covenant giving applies
- Auction proceeds route: DataOne retains processing margin, covenant 20–30% goes to foundation
- Winning bidder receives receipt + "I was there" badge for this auction in their impact feed
- Merchant receives full auction summary with per-item breakdown in their dashboard

#### Item Types
| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Product | Physical item from merchant inventory | Signed guitar, gift basket |
| Service | Redeemable service experience | Haircut, massage, dinner |
| Experience | One-of-a-kind moment | Private concert, behind-the-scenes tour |
| Collaboration bundle | Items from multiple certified merchants | "Date night" = dinner + flowers + show tickets |
| Artist item | Offered by the performing artist | Handwritten lyrics, signed setlist, private lesson |

### Integration with Live Shows
When an auction runs alongside a live show:
- Auction sidebar in the viewer's app is synchronized with the main auction system
- Artist's performer dashboard shows live auction status
- Closing call: in the final 5 minutes, the giving meter shows the auction's current contribution
- Artist can verbally call items — "We have $75 on the dinner, who's going to take it to $100?" — because they can see current high bids
- This is the mechanic no other platform offers: a real auctioneer who is also the entertainer, calling items in real time to their own audience

## 4. The Three-Way Economic Split

### The Split Structure

```
Every dollar that flows through a TheGiveback Live event:

Ticket Revenue or Tip Amount ($10.00 example)
   └── Artist: 70%  →  $7.00  (released within 2 business days)
   └── Foundation: 20%  →  $2.00  (added to monthly disbursement)
   └── Platform: 10%  →  $1.00  (covers DataOne processing costs)

Auction Bid ($100.00 example)
   └── DataOne acquiring margin: ~1.45%  →  ~$1.45
       └── Covenant 20–30%  →  ~$0.36  (to foundation, from margin)
   └── Merchant receives: item value recognized
   └── Winner receives: item + "I was there" badge
   └── Foundation: separate from the margin covenant — auction bid itself
       goes to the merchant who donated the item, not to the foundation
       directly. The foundation receives from the acquiring covenant.
```

### Why 70/20/10

**70% to artist:** Below StageIt's 80% but meaningfully above Twitch's 50%. Artists who use TheGiveback Live are self-selecting for mission alignment — the 10% difference from StageIt's maximum is the price of being part of something bigger, having a cause story, and having the certified merchant network behind them. Artists who want maximum payout should use StageIt. Artists who want their music to mean something in their community should use TheGiveback Live.

**20% to foundation:** Significant enough to be meaningful for foundations (a show with $500 in tips generates $100 for the cause — real money for a local food pantry). Not so large that artists feel they're working for charity. The foundation receives this alongside the regular monthly covenant giving from the merchant host's transaction volume — so a show is additive, not substitutive.

**10% to platform:** Covers DataOne's payment processing cost. No profit extracted from the giving event. This is important for the covenant integrity — the platform fee is a cost recovery, not a margin center.

### Two Separate Ledger Pools (Always)

As with all TheGiveback giving, the show's foundation contribution appears in the ledger as a separate line item labeled by its source:
- "Concert contribution — [Artist Name] show at [Merchant Name] — [Date]"
- Never merged with the merchant's covenant margin contribution
- Foundation sees both streams separately on their disbursement report

### Tip Tax Treatment
*(Not legal advice — confirm with tax counsel before launch)*

Tips from fans to artists via the platform may be taxable income to the artist. Artists receive a 1099-K if their annual tip income exceeds the IRS threshold. The platform's 10% fee is treated as a payment processing fee, not a deduction from a charitable contribution. The foundation's 20% receives the same DAF routing treatment as all other TheGiveback consumer-directed giving — routed through the Givehub partnership or DAF intermediary before reaching the foundation.

## 5. The Community Layer — Impact Feed

### What It Is

A verified testimony layer inside the consumer mobile app. Not a social network. Not a content feed. A place where the giving record generates content automatically and people add their voice to what actually happened.

The feed starts full from day one — populated by the platform's own ledger events. It does not depend on users to create content to be useful.

### Three-Tab Structure

#### Tab 1: My Impact
*Personal giving journal — writes itself*

A chronological timeline of every giving moment connected to the consumer's own verified behavior:
- Every purchase at a certified merchant (pulled from transaction history)
- Every tip at a TheGiveback Live show
- Every round-up at checkout
- Every Giveback Points donation to a foundation

Each item shows:
```
Miller's Hardware  ·  June 14
Your purchase contributed to the Midtown Food Pantry.
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Miller's total this month: $412
[See what $412 did →]  [I witness this]
```

When a foundation publishes an outcome post, it links back to every consumer who contributed to that disbursement. "You were part of this."

Design rules:
- Consumer never needs to post, share, or do anything to populate this tab
- Every entry is anchored to a verified transaction — no claimed giving, only documented giving
- Tab is private by default — consumer can choose to make specific entries public

#### Tab 2: My Community
*Neighborhood bulletin board for giving activity*

Chronological feed. No algorithm. Local by default (configurable radius, default 10 miles). Shows:

| Content Type | Example | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Merchant giving post | "Miller's Hardware gave $412 to Midtown Food Pantry this month" | Auto-generated from ledger, merchant can add caption |
| Foundation outcome post | "Your neighborhood's $2,847 funded 632 meals in June" | Foundation publishes manually via Givehub portal |
| Show announcement | "Friday Night Live at Miller's Hardware · Sarah J · 7pm" | Auto-generated when show is published |
| Show recap | "Last night's show raised $623 for Midtown Food Pantry" | Auto-generated from show settlement |
| New certification | "[New Merchant] just became Giveback Certified in [neighborhood]" | Auto-generated from certification event |
| New foundation | "[Foundation] joined the network — now receiving support from [X] merchants" | Auto-generated from foundation approval |

Design rules:
- No follower/following model — every certified merchant and foundation in the radius appears
- No algorithmic ranking — pure chronological
- No cross-posting or internal repost mechanic
- Tab shows only verified events — not user-generated claims

#### Tab 3: Moments
*Live event discovery*

No algorithmic boost. No "promoted" shows. No discovery fee from merchants or artists.

### The Three Engagement Mechanics

Replace the standard social "like" with three intentional responses:

#### "I Witness This"
- Equivalent to a like in function, but language that carries meaning
- You are testifying to having seen the good — not just approving it
- Available on: merchant giving posts, foundation outcome posts, show recaps
- Display: "47 witnesses" not "47 likes"
- Technical: stored as a `witness` event on the ledger entry, not a separate social action

#### "I Was There"
- Available only to consumers whose transaction history includes a purchase at that merchant within the giving period
- Verified badge — cannot be tapped by anyone who wasn't actually a customer
- Display: "12 were there" — shown differently from witnesses, as a separate count
- This is the mechanic no other social platform can offer — it is backed by payment data
- Implementation: a consumer taps "I was there" on a merchant's June giving post. The platform queries the ledger for any transaction by this user at this merchant in June. If found: badge granted. If not found: button disabled with tooltip "You haven't shopped here recently."

#### "I Give Too"
- Tapping this triggers an immediate Giveback Points donation to the merchant's current matched foundation, equal to whatever the smallest tip amount is for the current show (or a fixed $1 for non-show posts)
- If no Giveback Points balance: prompts the consumer to add a $1 round-up via the round-up flow
- Converts social engagement directly into giving in one tap
- Display: "23 gave too — $23 added to [Foundation Name]"
- This appears on the foundation's disbursement as "Community Feed contributions — [Date]" — separate from merchant covenant giving

### Merchant Posts

Auto-generated monthly. Merchant can customize caption before posting (or let it post automatically). Example:

**Auto-generated content:**
> Miller's Hardware · June 2026
> $412 given to Midtown Food Pantry
> From 847 transactions · 47 witnesses · 12 were there

**Merchant can add:**
> "June was our best giving month yet. Thank you to everyone who shopped with us — you made this happen. — Tom"

**Sharing:** One-tap share to Instagram, Facebook, or copy link. The shared post includes a link back to the public ledger entry. The merchant's own social audience sees the giving post with a verification link — not a claim, a proof.

Design rules for merchant posts:
- Amount is the actual disbursed amount — never projected or estimated
- Post does not appear in the feed until the disbursement is confirmed
- Merchant cannot edit the dollar amount — only the caption
- No merchant can see another merchant's exact dollar amount in the feed (they see the post, not the underlying transaction volume that generated it)

### Foundation Outcome Posts

Foundation publishes these manually through the Givehub portal. Required fields:
- Period covered (month and year)
- Total received from TheGiveback network
- Specific outcome: what did the money fund? (Required — vague "thank you" posts rejected)
- Evidence: at minimum one specific metric ("632 meals," "14 families housed," "87 youth served")

Example outcome post:
> Midtown Food Pantry · June 2026
> Received $2,847 from TheGiveback network
> What it funded: 632 meals at our Friday distribution, plus 3 months of refrigeration repair costs
> 12 merchants contributed · 23 community members witnessed

When this post publishes, it appears in the My Impact tab of every consumer whose transactions contributed to that disbursement during the month. Notification: "See what your shopping did."

### Community Giving Streak (Phase 4)

When the Giveback Card exists and transaction data is richer, build a neighborhood-level streak:

- Tracks consecutive months where the certified merchant community in a defined geography (zip code or neighborhood) has given continuously
- Displayed on the local foundation's impact page and in the My Community feed
- "Your neighborhood has supported Midtown Food Pantry for 14 consecutive months"
- This is collective identity, not individual competition
- No individual-level streaks — only community-level
- Celebrated by the foundation, the merchants, and the consumers together

### What Is Deliberately Excluded

| Feature | Why Excluded |
|---|---|
| Follower / following model | Creates status hierarchies — merchants with more followers have more visible giving. Violates widow's mite principle. |
| Comments on merchant giving posts | Requires moderation. Can go negative. Creates maintenance burden before platform has resources. Phase 4 consideration only, and only on foundation posts. |
| Internal repost / quote mechanic | The feed doesn't go viral internally. Growth comes from external sharing (Instagram, Facebook) via the share button on individual posts. |
| Public leaderboards | "Top giving merchant this month" corrupts the mission — merchants start gaming for position rather than giving from conviction. |
| Algorithmic feed ranking | Algorithmic feeds create winners and losers. Chronological + local respects every merchant equally. |
| User-generated content unrelated to giving | This is not a lifestyle social network. Content is generated by verified giving events. |
| Profile photos / social profiles | Merchants have storefronts. Consumers have impact feeds. Neither needs a social profile with followers. |

4. `shows.foundation_id` must reference an approved foundation — foreign key with status check constraint
5. `shows.merchant_id` must reference a certified merchant — foreign key with certification status check

## 7. Technical Stack & Integration Points

### Video Streaming Layer

**Use Mux or Cloudflare Stream — do not build streaming infrastructure.**

Rationale: video CDN, adaptive bitrate encoding, latency optimization, and recording are solved problems. The platform's value is in the economic architecture around the video, not the video itself. Both Mux and Cloudflare Stream offer:
- Live stream ingest via RTMP (artist streams from OBS, Streamlabs, or iPhone/Android)
- HLS delivery with adaptive bitrate
- Simultaneous viewer counts via webhooks
- Optional recording to object storage (off by default — see Section 9)
- Latency: 3–8 seconds (sufficient for a live music show — no need for sub-second WebRTC latency)

**Integration point:** On show start, platform creates a Mux live stream asset, stores the stream key in `shows.stream_url`, and returns the playback URL to the viewer app. Mux sends webhooks on stream start/end/disconnection. On stream end, platform triggers show settlement.

### Payment Processing

All ticket purchases, tips, and auction bids flow through DataOne's existing acquiring infrastructure. No new payment processor needed. The platform adds:
- Split calculation service (runs before settlement — calculates artist/foundation/platform shares)
- Payout scheduler (artist payout 2 business days post-show via existing ACH)
- Foundation contribution routing to the Givehub API (same as all other TheGiveback giving)

### Real-Time Communication

Tip messages and auction bid updates need real-time delivery during shows. Use **WebSockets via Pusher or Ably** — hosted services that handle connection management and scaling. Do not build a WebSocket server.

Events pushed in real time:
- New tip received (amount, message, running total)

## 9. What Not to Build

| What | Why Not |
|---|---|
| Own streaming infrastructure | Solved problem. Mux and Cloudflare Stream are cheaper and better than anything built in-house. |
| Show archives by default | Scarcity drives live attendance. Default: no archive. Artists can opt into a 24-hour replay window. The live moment — including auction close and giving meter milestone — is the product. |
| General-purpose social profiles | Merchants have storefronts. Consumers have impact feeds. Adding social profiles with photos and bios creates a maintenance and moderation burden without adding value to the giving mission. |
| Algorithmic content ranking | Algorithms create winners and losers. Chronological + local gives every certified merchant equal visibility regardless of their social following. |
| Follower / following system | Status hierarchies violate the widow's mite principle. Every merchant's giving is equally visible. |
| Individual leaderboards | "Top giving merchant" or "most generous fan" corrupt the mission. Giving is not a competition. |
| Internal content reposting | The feed doesn't go viral internally. Growth comes from external sharing to existing social platforms. |
| Comments on merchant giving posts | Moderation requirement before the platform has the resources. Phase 5 consideration only. |
| Payment processing outside DataOne | Every dollar at every event flows through DataOne's existing rails. No Stripe, no PayPal, no Square for events. |
| Discovery fees for promoted shows | No merchant can pay to surface their show higher in the discovery feed. The feed is chronological and local only. |

## 10. Success Metrics

### Live Events

| Metric | Target | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Shows hosted per month per market | 5+ per metro by month 12 | Shows the merchant network is engaged |
| Average giving per show (foundation share) | $75+ per show | Validates the economic model for foundations |
| Artist return rate | 60%+ play a second show | Artists who return believe in the platform |
| Fan attendance-to-tip conversion | 40%+ of attendees tip | Shows giving behavior is normalized, not exceptional |
| Auction close rate (% of items that receive bids) | 80%+ | Validates auction item quality and bidder engagement |

### Silent Auction

| Metric | Target | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Auctions hosted per month | Growing month over month | Adoption rate across merchant base |
| Average items per auction | 8–12 | Enough variety to drive competitive bidding |
| Bid-to-view ratio | 15%+ of auction page views result in a bid | Engagement quality |
| Cross-merchant item contribution rate | 30%+ of auctions have items from 2+ merchants | Validates the network collaboration mechanic |

### Community Layer

| Metric | Target | Why |
|---|---|---|
| "I was there" verification rate | 30%+ of eligible users tap it | Verified engagement > claimed engagement |
| "I give too" conversion rate | 15%+ of witnesses | Social engagement converting to giving |

| External share rate (Instagram/Facebook) | 20%+ of merchant posts shared externally | Platform content driving external awareness |

### The One Metric That Matters Above All

**Do artists come back because they believe the giving is real?**

If artists return for a second show, it is because the platform delivered on its promise — the giving meter was real, the foundation contribution posted publicly, the community showed up. An artist return rate above 60% is the most meaningful signal that TheGiveback Live is doing what it says it does.

## 11. Fixed Decisions for This Feature

| Decision | Rationale |
|---|---|
| Split is 70/20/10 (artist/foundation/platform) | Mission-aligned but sustainable. Not negotiable per show. |
| Foundation 20% routes through the same Givehub / DAF structure as all other TheGiveback giving | Legal consistency — consumer-directed funds need the same DAF protection as round-up funds. |
| No show archives by default | Scarcity is a feature. Artists opt in to 24-hour replay. |
| No algorithmic feed | Equal visibility for all certified merchants. Chronological + local only. |
| No follower / following model | Violates widow's mite principle. Every merchant has equal standing. |
| "I was there" requires transaction verification | This mechanic only works if it's real. Unverified versions are meaningless. |
| No leaderboards of any kind | No "top giving merchant," no "most generous fan." Giving is not a competition. |
| Artist cannot go live without merchant host confirmation | The merchant is the accountable party in the DataOne relationship. Artists cannot create events without a certified merchant behind them. |
| All payments through DataOne | Every ticket, tip, and auction bid is a DataOne transaction. No third-party processors for events. |
| The community layer is inside the consumer app, not a separate app | Events and community reinforce the same daily behavior — browse local certified merchants, attend their shows, see their giving. One app, not two. |
| Comments not enabled in Phase 3 or 4 | The platform cannot be moderated at scale before it reaches scale. Foundation outcome posts may enable comments in Phase 5. |
