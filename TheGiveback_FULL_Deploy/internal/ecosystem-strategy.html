The Full Circle Ecosystem: What to Build and Why
The e-commerce marketplace isn't a fourth product — it's the layer that closes the loop between acquiring, issuing, giving, and community commerce. When built correctly, every transaction inside the network generates revenue on both sides, drives more giving, and keeps money circulating among the same certified merchants and foundations.
The closed-loop insight
What Block/Square proved — and what TheGiveback does differently

Block's greatest structural insight: sitting on both the acquiring side (Square — merchants accept payments) and the issuing side (Cash App — consumers spend with their card) means transactions between their own merchants and cardholders create a closed loop. At scale, Block earns ~3% on closed-loop transactions vs ~1% on open-loop. TheGiveback builds this same closed loop — but every incremental dollar of economics it captures goes directly into foundation giving, not just profits.

Block / Square
Square acquires merchant transactions. Cash App issues cards to consumers. When both sides transact together, closed-loop economics = ~3x open-loop revenue. Mission: financial access.

Bilt Rewards
Issued cards for rent payments (near-zero interchange). Funded rich travel rewards from thin margin. Economics never closed. Wells Fargo lost $10M/month. Lesson: closed loop only works when the underlying transaction generates interchange.

DataOne acquires merchant-present card transactions (high interchange). Giveback Card issues to consumers who spend at certified merchants. Closed loop on the highest-interchange transaction category. Every incremental dollar funds more giving.

Four non-negotiable design decisions
Never violate this
Don't compete with your merchants
The marketplace lists and sells merchant products — TheGiveback never sells its own. Amazon destroyed merchant trust by becoming a seller. You are infrastructure, not a competitor. This constraint is permanent and non-negotiable regardless of scale.
Never violate this
Zero platform fee to certified merchants
You earn acquiring margin on every marketplace transaction. You don't need a listing fee on top. Adding a platform fee turns an asset (free distribution) into an obstacle. Merchants stay because the marketplace generates revenue for them, not because you're extracting it from them.
Marketplace before card
The Giveback Card needs certified merchant storefronts to be meaningful to cardholders. Without a network of local merchants where they earn bonus points, there's no reason to carry the card. Build the marketplace (Phase 3) before pitching BaaS partners (Phase 4).
Keep points inside the ecosystem
Points can be donated to foundations or redeemed at certified merchants. Not transferred to airlines. Not freely cashed out. Money that flows to certified merchants as discounts spins back through the acquiring engine, generating more giving per dollar of value distributed.
Build sequence — what to build when
✓
Now / Phase 1–2
Prove the plumbing — ledger, accrual, disbursement
The acquiring engine, covenant, and public ledger. First real disbursement must complete before any marketplace or storefront work begins. The counter on the website must show real dollars.
3
Phase 3 · Months 5–8
Certified merchant storefronts + marketplace launch
Simple product/service listing for existing certified merchants. DataOne processes all transactions. Consumers can browse by zip code and buy local. Every transaction generates the giving story. No new payment infrastructure needed — DataOne already processes it.
4
Phase 4 · Months 9–14
Giveback Points layer (card-linked, no branded card yet)
Enable Giveback Points on marketplace purchases using any existing card via Pledge or similar card-linking API. Consumers accumulate points on certified merchant transactions and can donate them to foundations. This generates behavioral data before committing to a full card program.
5
Phase 5 · Months 14–24
Giveback Card launch (BaaS + closed loop)
By this point: live merchant network, real giving track record, behavioral data from points program. The BaaS pitch to Marqeta or Galileo is now backed by evidence. Card launches to waitlist of consumers who've already engaged. Closed loop begins. Giving per transaction roughly doubles.
The flywheel, stated plainly

More merchants join DataOne because of the Giveback certification → their storefronts attract consumers to the marketplace → consumers discover the Giveback Card to earn points at those merchants → closed-loop transactions generate acquiring and issuing revenue → more giving flows to foundations → foundations promote the certified merchants in their community networks → more merchants join.

Every rotation of the flywheel generates more giving, not less. It's the opposite of most loyalty economics, which get more expensive as they scale. This one gets cheaper to operate and more impactful per transaction as the network grows.
